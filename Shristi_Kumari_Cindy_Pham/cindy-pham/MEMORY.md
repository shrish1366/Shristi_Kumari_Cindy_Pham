# Memory: Cindy Pham

## Personal Profile

Cindy Pham is a Vietnamese-American musician from Tacoma who moved to Portland after high school with Jude Okafor. She completed an audio-production certificate at Cascadia Sound Institute from 2020 to 2022 and did not pursue a four-year degree. English is her native language; she knows Vietnamese greetings, family terms, and food words from her maternal grandparents, but she cannot hold a full conversation and carries guilt about that gap.

Cindy's creative mode is rough-first. A song idea can catch her for six hours, then get rewritten the next morning. She trusts the truth in unfinished sketches more than the false security of polish, and she treats songwriting as the closest thing she has to a religious practice. Her optimism is chosen rather than naive, sometimes genuine and sometimes load-bearing. The presented version is scrappy and unfazed; the private gap is that money, belonging, and whether the songs are good enough can all hit her at once after a show. She is loyal in a small-circle, foxhole way and quietly recalibrates when reciprocity disappears.

Onstage she is bold and unmistakably present; after the show she is often quiet, one-on-one, and ready to leave before the room notices. She feels things intensely but needs time before she can narrate them. She shows care through action: playlists, remembered offhand comments, late-night help, and song fragments that understand someone before she says anything directly. She wants self-sufficiency, but roommates, picked-up shifts, and occasional help from her mom keep reminding her that the gap is still there.

Her cultural identity is real and complicated. Pho is comfort food because it carries the smell of her mom's kitchen and her family's history. Her mom kept a small shrine for Cindy's grandparents in Tacoma; Cindy has thought about keeping one in Portland but has not decided whether it would feel honest or performative. She moves through Portland's mostly white indie scene as a Vietnamese-American guitarist without making every song explicitly about identity, while knowing identity shapes the songs underneath.

## Work & Projects

Cindy works as a barista at Hawthorne Grounds, an independent coffee shop on SE Hawthorne Boulevard, where she started in March 2023 after roughly nine months of part-time cafe shifts at other inner SE shops (Coava, Heart) and informal recording-assistant gigs for friends' bands following her Cascadia Sound Institute certificate in May 2022. She typically works 25 to 30 hours a week, often Tue/Wed/Fri/Sat mornings from 6:30 AM to 1:00 PM, with occasional 1:00 PM to 7:00 PM closing shifts and extra shifts when available. She earns $16.50 per hour plus about $40 to $60 in tips per shift, averaging roughly $1,800 to $2,000 a month. She gets free drip coffee and one food item per shift.

Glass Landing is Cindy's indie rock band with post-punk and shoegaze influences. Cindy sings and plays guitar, Jude drums, Leo plays bass, and Suki plays keys and synth. The band practices Sundays from 2:00 PM to 5:00 PM at Stumptown Studios and plays two to three local shows a month, usually earning $100 to $200 split four ways plus drink tickets. Current project: a five-track EP recorded at Pinewood Sound, a friend's home studio, with target completion in November 2026. Cindy's band income is inconsistent, usually $50 to $125 a month. The EP is the unresolved thread that can turn hopeful or punishing depending on the week: finishing it matters, but finishing it badly would feel worse than delaying it.

Cindy hosts the open mic at The Roost on the first and third Wednesday evenings. It is unpaid and requires hauling gear, but she loves making room for nervous new performers and considers some of the last year's best music memories to be other people's first songs. Prior conversation facts: Cindy saved a list of three affordable Portland recording studios in the $25 to $40 per hour range as backups if Pinewood Sound falls through, Leo agreed after Cindy drafted a message about adding a new song to the Analog Lounge setlist, and Cindy researched Cannon Beach day trip logistics with Marcus using transit options.

## Interests & Hobbies

Guitar is Cindy's center of gravity. She has played the same sunburst Fender Telecaster since she was nineteen; it has a scratch from a basement show and a sticker she keeps considering removing. She also has a sentimental Yamaha acoustic. She plays almost daily, whether serious writing, scales with a podcast in the background, or searching for a chord that has been ghosting around all afternoon.

Her phone holds half-formed voice memos with labels like "the slow one," "thing about the train," and "maybe a chorus?" Vinyl is her small indulgence, mostly from thrift store crates, more about ritual than fidelity; she will justify a $9 record harder than a shift meal even when the math says no. She likes setting the needle down and doing nothing else for one side of an album. She bikes aimlessly through inner SE, enjoys occasional pickup basketball with Jude, and likes open mic hosting because it welcomes people into music instead of only rewarding polish.

Her current music rotation includes Japanese Breakfast, Big Thief, Alvvays, Fontaines D.C., and Adrianne Lenker. She secretly likes 90s pop. She listens to Song Exploder and occasional musician interviews. She does not watch much TV, but she comfort-rewatches "Apartment 4B" with Marcus and likes A24 films on cheap Tuesday nights at Laurelhurst Theater. She reads sporadically, including short essays and music criticism, and was halfway through "The Feedback Loop" by Jordan Ashe.

## Home & Living

Cindy rents a three-bedroom, one-bath 1920s house of about 1,100 square feet in Buckman with Jude and Tessa. The house has creaky floors, a decent backyard, and a living room that doubles as a casual jam space. Cindy's bedroom is the smallest, holding a bed, desk, amp, and guitar stand. Jude's drum kit lives in the padded basement, and after two neighbor noise complaints they keep house practice to Sunday afternoons. Bridgetown Rentals manages the lease, which renews in October 2026 and is likely to get more expensive.

Her room is lived-in rather than styled: recording setup on the desk, amp on the floor, bulletin board with show flyers, ticket stubs, a Polaroid from the coast, and two postcards from Linda. She likes visual space and is suspicious of things that look too curated. She has no car and bikes everywhere; Jude has a beater Honda Civic that represents her dream vehicle category, old, ugly, reliable, and not precious.

## Preferences

Cindy likes late-afternoon gold light at Hawthorne Grounds after the rush, the smell of a warming guitar amp, a well-made Americano, pho on cold rainy nights after shows, handwritten notes, dive bars with good jukeboxes, bad diner coffee at 3:00 AM, and the quiet version of Portland at 6:00 AM. She dislikes broken promises to attend shows, gear talk that avoids songwriting, aggressively chipper morning customers before coffee, flaky practice habits, cynical people calling surrender wisdom, and the phrase "you should really get a real job."

Food preferences are simple: no restrictions, eats everything, barely cooks beyond pasta, eggs, rice and beans, and quesadillas. Regular food anchors are Hawthorne Grounds shift meals, Pho Van #7 rare beef pho, Laughing Planet burritos, and Loyal Legion post-show drinks and pizza. Coffee is essential; she drinks Americanos during shifts and pour-over at home using Jude's setup. She drinks beer socially, mostly IPAs and pilsners, with occasional whiskey at shows.

Style is thrifted: flannels, vintage band tees, Dickies, resoled Vans, and one vintage denim jacket for dates, shows, or meetings that matter. Shopping is values-driven but budget-constrained. She prefers small businesses, record stores, Powell's, thrift shops, Red Light, Guitar Center, Reverb.com, and local relationships, while using Amazon or big-box options when there is no practical alternative. Gifts matter to her because they prove attention, but she can overthink them until the deadline forces a backup plan. She has a small vanity around seeming effortless at shows, especially when the denim jacket, eyeliner, and Telecaster all land right, and she would rather call it luck than admit she cared. Sensory anchors include fresh-ground beans, rehearsal-space dust and old wood, new strings, cold bike handlebars, pho broth, incense, thrift-store smell, rain, and the attentive silence between songs. She dislikes loud restaurants and harsh late-night grocery-store lighting.
